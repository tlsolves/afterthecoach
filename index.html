Understand the model Why sustained leader engagement is a design problem, not a motivation problem
The mechanism: structured participation + explicit metrics
TL's model treats leader engagement as something that has to be designed into the partnership structure — not hoped for. That means setting a concrete participation target before the work begins (30–35% of coaching visits), anchoring PL and coaching to the standards and frameworks leaders already use, and providing real-time side-by-side coaching during PLCs rather than just pre/post briefings.
Why this is a coherence problem
Leaders disengage when PL feels separate from coaching, and coaching feels separate from their evaluation responsibilities. When all three are explicitly connected — using the same language, the same tools, the same priorities — leaders start to see participation as part of their role, not an add-on to it.

What TL did
Codified leadership learning community
Coached Principal Flores through structured PLC facilitation
Real-time side-by-side coaching during PLCs — not just pre/post briefings — built capacity faster. The focus was data analysis tied directly to Bluebonnet HQIM priorities, using TPESS 5.4 and the PLC Vertical Rubric as shared metrics.
Leadership engagement with JESP
Leaders observed and debriefed alongside TL coaches
Joint observation cycles using Spot Check and TTESS gave leaders a structured lens for classroom visits. After coaching, leaders independently identified 2 of 3 effective facilitation actions — a measurable shift from baseline.
The artifact that made it work
Tools that connect PL to coaching
The Alignment Crosswalk mapped the whole system in one place
TPESS principal standards → teacher practice (TTESS/Spots) → student outcomes (DOLs, Unit Assessments, STAAR Blueprint) — all in one coherent artifact. Leaders could see how their participation connected to classroom results, making the model legible rather than just logical. A 47% reduction in schools with no evidence of curriculum routines followed.

The model · Organizational leadership · Tiara Toney
The conditions that make
coherence possible
TL's integrated model isn't a curriculum or a program — it's a set of design principles for how professional learning and job-embedded coaching can be deliberately connected so that leaders build lasting capacity, not just temporary compliance.
Tiara Toney · section lead
Four design principles
Design principle 01
Coherence is designed, not assumed
PL and coaching are aligned by explicit intention — the same priorities appear in learning sessions and in coaching moves. TL builds that alignment before a cycle begins, not after.
Content to expand · Tiara
Design principle 02
Leaders are learners, not just sponsors
TL's model positions school and district leaders as active participants in professional learning — not approvers of it. That participation is structured, measured, and expected from day one.
Content to expand · Tiara
Design principle 03
Sustainability is built in from the start
The model is designed to transfer ownership to leaders over time. Not as a hope — as a planned design feature with explicit handoff structures and internal capacity targets.
Content to expand · Tiara
Design principle 04
Problem first, model second
This site leads with practitioner problems, not TL's approach. Districts and leaders are the primary actors — TL's model is the supporting context, not the headline.
