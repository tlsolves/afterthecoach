Coaching ends.
Does the work?
Every JESP faces the same question: what happens to leaders' instructional capacity after direct support ends? Teaching Lab built a model to answer it — connecting whole-group professional learning and job-embedded coaching so that districts can sustain math improvement independently. Here's what we learned across four districts, and what any JESP can use.
For JESP providers and district leaders · CPRL Convening 2 · June 2026
TL's answer to the network's core question: how do JESPs build leadership capacity that outlasts direct support?
The CPRL network is asking what practices are worth sustaining, what districts can own, and what lighter-touch models look like. This site documents TL's approach to all three — grounded in evidence from La Joya ISD (Texas), District 75, Transfer High Schools, and DCPS.
What works
Structured leader participation + explicit metrics + aligned tools. La Joya shows measurable leader movement from this combination.
What sustains
Coherence between PL and coaching — deliberately designed before the cycle begins, not retrofitted after.
What districts own
The data, the tools, and the facilitation routines — transferred by design through explicit handoff structures.
Lighter-touch model
Artifacts and protocols that leaders use independently — so the coaching relationship doesn't have to carry everything.

Understand the model Why sustained leader engagement is a design problem, not a motivation problem
The mechanism: structured participation + explicit metrics
TL's model treats leader engagement as something that has to be designed into the partnership structure — not hoped for. That means setting a concrete participation target before the work begins (30–35% of coaching visits), anchoring PL and coaching to the standards and frameworks leaders already use, and providing real-time side-by-side coaching during PLCs rather than just pre/post briefings.
Why this is a coherence problem
Leaders disengage when PL feels separate from coaching, and coaching feels separate from their evaluation responsibilities. When all three are explicitly connected — using the same language, the same tools, the same priorities — leaders start to see participation as part of their role, not an add-on to it.

The model · Organizational leadership
The conditions that make
coherence possible
TL's integrated model isn't a curriculum or a program — it's a set of deliberate design choices for how professional learning and job-embedded coaching connect so that leaders build lasting capacity, not just temporary compliance. These principles are context-independent: they traveled from La Joya, Texas to D75, NYC, and they can travel further.
Tiara Toney · section lead
This section documents TL's organizational approach — the layer above individual case studies that makes the model replicable. It answers: what did TL deliberately build, and what can any JESP adapt?
The CPRL hypothesis this model is built to test
If school and district leaders actively and consistently participate in curriculum-aligned professional learning communities and structured observation cycles, then they will be able to independently reinforce instructional priorities and begin to create a math learning community that sustains coaching impact beyond direct JESP support.
The operative word is independently. The design principles below are how TL makes that independence achievable — not hoped for.
Five design principles
Design principle 01
Coherence is designed, not assumed
PL and coaching are aligned by explicit intention before a cycle begins — not retrofitted after the fact. The same priorities, language, and look-fors appear in whole-group learning sessions and in coaching move logs. When leaders encounter the same framework in both spaces, the model becomes legible. When they don't, it fragments. TL designs the connection deliberately: every PL session maps forward to the coaching cycle that follows it, and every coaching debrief references back to the PL session it came from.
Convening 2 · What sustains
Design principle 02
Leaders are learners, not just sponsors
TL's model positions school and district leaders as active participants in professional learning — not approvers of it. That participation is structured, measured, and expected from day one of the partnership. In La Joya, this meant coaching Principal Flores through PLC facilitation in real time, not briefing her before and after. In D75 — a New York City school district — it meant setting a concrete 30–35% coaching visit presence target before the year began. Leader engagement isn't a precondition — it's something TL designs for and holds accountable.
Convening 2 · What works
Design principle 03
Sustainability is built in from the start
TL designs explicitly for handoff — not as a hope at the end of the year, but as a planned design feature from day one. That means naming which routines leaders will own independently by year's end, scaffolding toward that transfer throughout the coaching cycle, and building the data habits that make independent monitoring possible. In La Joya, the transfer was real: the work continued after direct TL support pulled back. The question TL asks at the start of every partnership is not "How do we support leaders?" but "How do we make ourselves unnecessary?"
Convening 2 · Lighter-touch model
Convening 2 · What sustains
Design principle 04
District ownership is the goal, not the byproduct
The tools, data, and facilitation routines TL builds are designed to transfer to district ownership — not to live in TL's systems. The JES tracker, the alignment crosswalk, the coaching move log: all are built so that district leaders can pick them up and use them without a TL coach in the room. This is what makes the model a lighter-touch investment over time: the infrastructure is inside the district, not dependent on the provider. TL's role shifts from doing to coaching leaders to do — and eventually to coaching them to coach others.
Convening 2 · What districts own
Design principle 05
Problem first, model second
This site leads with the problems practitioners are already naming — not TL's model, not a theory of action. That's a deliberate choice about how knowledge travels. A JESP or school leader who recognizes a problem they're living is more likely to engage with the evidence than one who lands on a methodology they haven't encountered. The model is the supporting context. The problem is the entry point. And the artifacts are the thing the practitioner leaves with — not a description of TL's approach, but a tool they can put in their own hands.
For the network · Cross-provider relevance
For JESP providers and district leaders
These principles aren't TL-specific — they're the context-independent layer of the model
The district-specific tools — TPESS in Texas, IM-aligned observation rubrics in New York — are examples, not the replicables. The replicables are these design principles. Any JESP implementing HQIM in any district can ask: Have we designed coherence between our PL and coaching, or are we assuming it? Are leaders learners in our model, or just sponsors? Have we named what the district will own when we leave? Those questions work regardless of curriculum, context, or cohort.
