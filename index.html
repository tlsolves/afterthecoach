Coaching ends.
Does the work?

Every instructional support provider faces the same question: what happens to leaders' instructional capacity after direct support ends? Teaching Lab built a model to answer it, connecting whole-group professional learning and job-embedded coaching so districts can sustain math improvement independently. Here's what we learned across four contexts, and what any provider can adapt.

For instructional support providers and district leaders
What works
Structured leader participation, explicit metrics, aligned tools, and evidence from La Joya ISD that the combination moves leaders measurably.
What sustains
Coherence between PL and coaching, designed deliberately before the cycle starts, not assumed after the fact.
What districts own
Data, tools, and facilitation routines transferred by design, so the coaching relationship doesn't have to carry everything.
What travels
Design principles that are context-independent, tested in Texas, New York City, and Washington D.C.
An honest starting point

This work is genuinely hard, and that's worth naming.

System leaders are being asked to sustain instructional improvement while simultaneously managing everything else that comes with running a district or school, staff crises, budget pressures, compliance demands, and the constant pull of urgent over important. The expectation that leaders will also show up consistently for coaching visits, lead professional learning, and give teachers real-time instructional feedback is a real ask, not a light one.

And the field has made it harder than it needed to be. Leaders are often asked to own instructional improvement without being given the tools, frameworks, or protected time to practice it. They're expected to step into classrooms and give meaningful feedback before they've ever had a low-stakes opportunity to try. They're asked to sustain what coaching started without a clear picture of what "sustaining" actually requires of them day to day.

The work on this site doesn't minimize that complexity. It was built in response to it, with the belief that when leaders have the right structures, shared tools, and explicit support for building their own instructional identity, the work becomes not just possible but sustainable.

Problem 01

Sustaining leader engagement
beyond the first session

Superintendent and principal participation is strongest at launch, and hardest to maintain over time. When engagement is structured into the partnership from day one, leaders build the habits that outlast direct support. This is a design problem, not a motivation problem.

Home› Browse by problem› Problem 01
Understand the model
The mechanism: structured participation and explicit metrics
TL's model treats leader engagement as something that has to be designed into the partnership structure, not hoped for. That means setting a concrete participation target before the work begins (30–35% of coaching visits), anchoring PL and coaching to the standards and frameworks leaders already use, and providing real-time side-by-side coaching during PLCs rather than just pre/post briefings.
Why this is a coherence problem, not a motivation problem
Leaders disengage when PL feels separate from coaching, and coaching feels separate from their evaluation responsibilities. When all three are explicitly connected, the same language, the same tools, the same priorities, leaders start to see participation as part of their role, not an add-on to it.
Tools to use
Alignment crosswalk
Maps principal standards → teacher practice → student outcomes. Gives leaders a visible reason to stay engaged by showing how their participation connects to classroom results. Adapt the framework structure to your district's evaluation language.
La Joya ISD
Leader engagement tracker
Tracks leader presence across coaching visits toward the 30–35% target. Shared with district leadership so participation becomes their data to act on, not just the instructional support provider's record to keep.

How can school leader coaching be structured across a district system to strengthen leadership practice, instructional coherence, and student outcomes?

Step 1 · Diagnose
What instructional, leadership, and system conditions must be understood to effectively align coaching support across the district?
We began by developing a deep understanding of the district context through a district-level needs assessment, leadership interviews, and artifact reviews to identify instructional priorities, leadership needs, and implementation gaps. School leaders also participated in joint learning walks, which served as both a diagnostic and relationship-building structure. Leaders observed instruction together, engaged in facilitated debrief conversations, and identified trends in instructional practice and areas for refinement across campuses.
Step 2 · Identify Metrics & Anchor the Work
What leadership, instructional, and student outcome metrics will anchor coaching, professional learning, and implementation monitoring across the system?
Using findings from the needs assessment and learning walks, we collaborated with district leaders to identify the metrics for leadership practice, teacher practice, and student performance that would anchor leadership coaching, professional learning, and progress monitoring across the system.
Leadership Practice
T-PESS
State principal evaluation rubric used to support leader self-reflection and identify leadership indicators most aligned to the project focus — specifically 5.4 (Data-Driven Instruction) and 2.3 (Observation & Feedback).
Teacher Practice
Spot Check + T-TESS
District observation tool aligned to the district's instructional lesson structure and T-TESS indicators, used to monitor instructional implementation and teacher practice across campuses.
Student Performance
DoLs · SCRs · Observational Evidence
District-created standards-aligned formative assessments including Demonstrations of Learning, Short Constructed Responses, and observational evidence of student thinking, writing, reading, and discourse.

Instructional Leadership Capacity Through Coaching
"We're not here to do all of the work for them. We're here to grow them so that they can actually do the work."
Coaches supporting La Joya engaged school leaders in modeling, rehearsal, and gradual release structures that strengthened leaders' ability to analyze instruction, facilitate feedback conversations, and lead instructional improvement independently. Over time, leaders developed greater confidence and instructional ownership, creating stronger long-term sustainability across campuses.
How
  • Build trust and identify leadership strengths and needs
  • Model coaching conversations and instructional feedback
  • Rehearse leader actions through role-play and walkthrough practice
  • Gradually release facilitation and coaching responsibilities
  • Monitor implementation and refine leadership practice through feedback
Systems Alignment Creates Coherent Leadership Practice
"Shared goals and instructional expectations created greater coherence across leadership actions, classroom instruction, and student outcomes."
Coaches supporting La Joya aligned leadership coaching, professional learning, instructional expectations, and district priorities into one coherent instructional improvement system. Shared frameworks, observation tools, curriculum expectations, and district metrics helped leaders connect classroom practice, PLC facilitation, teacher coaching, and student outcomes.
How
  • Identify district instructional priorities and success metrics
  • Align coaching goals to school and district expectations
  • Narrow priorities into high-leverage instructional practices
  • Reinforce shared look-fors across PLCs, walkthroughs, and coaching
  • Revisit alignment through implementation evidence and feedback
Data-Driven Coaching Cycles Improve Instructional Decision-Making
"Data was at the center of the coaching… it led every decision, every conversation."
Coaches supporting La Joya grounded coaching cycles in student performance, walkthrough evidence, lesson implementation, and teacher practice data to drive instructional decisions. Leaders used observable evidence and student outcomes to identify priorities, monitor implementation, and determine next instructional steps.
How
  • Analyze student and teacher performance data
  • Identify one high-leverage instructional focus area
  • Develop action steps tied to implementation evidence
  • Monitor instruction through walkthroughs and PLCs
  • Reflect on impact and adjust coaching priorities
Shared Coaching Goals Strengthen Systemwide Coherence
"The overarching goals that the district wanted… aligned to their accountability… it just makes the most sense."
Coaches supporting La Joya established common instructional priorities across schools to strengthen alignment between district initiatives, leadership coaching, PLCs, and teacher professional learning. Shared goals and instructional expectations created greater coherence across leadership actions, classroom instruction, and student outcomes.
How
  • Establish shared instructional priorities across schools
  • Align PLCs, walkthroughs, and coaching to common goals
  • Reinforce consistent instructional language and look-fors
  • Monitor implementation across leadership teams and classrooms
  • Adjust supports while maintaining districtwide coherence
Prepared Coaching Anticipates Barriers and Sustains Momentum
"You have to go in with a blank slate… understanding who the individual is, and how you can get them to grow."
Coaches supporting La Joya proactively anticipated implementation barriers, sequenced instructional priorities, and adjusted supports based on leader readiness and school context. This level of preparation helped sustain momentum, maintain instructional focus, and respond effectively to changing campus needs throughout the year.
How
  • Analyze school context and leadership readiness
  • Anticipate instructional and implementation barriers
  • Prepare differentiated supports and rehearsal opportunities
  • Monitor risks during observations and coaching conversations
  • Adjust coaching plans while maintaining focus on long-term goals

The model · Organizational leadership

The conditions that make
coherence possible

TL's integrated model isn't a curriculum or a program, it's a set of deliberate design choices for how professional learning and coaching connect so that leaders build lasting capacity. These principles traveled from Texas to New York City. They can travel further.

The CPRL hypothesis this model is built to test
If school and district leaders actively and consistently participate in curriculum-aligned professional learning communities and structured observation cycles, then they will be able to independently reinforce instructional priorities and sustain coaching impact beyond direct instructional support provider support.
The operative word is independently. The design principles below are how TL makes that independence achievable, not hoped for.
Five design principles
01
Coherence
Coherence is designed, not assumed
PL and coaching are aligned by explicit intention before a cycle begins, not retrofitted after the fact. The same priorities, language, and look-fors appear in whole-group learning sessions and in coaching move logs. When leaders encounter the same framework in both spaces, the model becomes legible. TL designs the connection deliberately: every PL session maps forward to the coaching cycle that follows it, and every coaching debrief references back to the PL session it came from.
What sustains
02
Participation
Leaders are learners, not just sponsors
TL's model positions school and district leaders as active participants in professional learning, not approvers of it. That participation is structured, measured, and expected from day one. In La Joya, this meant coaching Principal Flores through PLC facilitation in real time, not briefing her before and after. In District 75, it meant setting a concrete 30–35% coaching visit presence target before the year began. Leader engagement isn't a precondition, it's something TL designs for and holds accountable.
What works
03
Sustainability
Sustainability is built in from the start
TL designs explicitly for handoff, not as a hope at the end of the year, but as a planned design feature from day one. That means naming which routines leaders will own independently by year's end, scaffolding toward that transfer throughout the coaching cycle, and building the data habits that make independent monitoring possible. The question TL asks at the start of every partnership is not "How do we support leaders?" but "How do we make ourselves unnecessary?"
Districts carry more over timeWhat sustains
04
Ownership
District ownership is the goal, not the byproduct
The tools, data, and facilitation routines TL builds are designed to transfer to district ownership, not to live in TL's systems. The implementation tracker, the alignment crosswalk, the coaching move log: all are built so that district leaders can pick them up and use them without a TL coach in the room. TL's role shifts from doing to coaching leaders to do, and eventually to coaching them to coach others.
What districts own
05
Entry point
Problem first, model second
This site leads with the challenges practitioners are already navigating, not TL's model. That's a deliberate choice about how knowledge travels. A coach, provider, or school leader who recognizes a challenge they're living is more likely to engage with the evidence than one who lands on a methodology they haven't encountered. The model is the supporting context. The problem is the entry point.
Any provider can adapt this
For instructional support providers and district leaders
These principles aren't TL-specific, they're the context-independent layer of the model
The district-specific tools, TPESS in Texas, IM-aligned observation rubrics in New York, are examples, not the replicables. The replicables are these design principles. Any instructional support provider implementing HQIM can ask: Have we designed coherence between our PL and coaching, or are we assuming it? Are leaders learners in our model, or just sponsors? Have we named what the district will own when we leave?
